# Value-Iteration-for-Optimal-Inventory-Management-Policy
This project solves an infinite-horizon discounted Markov Decision Process to determine the optimal weekly ordering policy for an inventory system.

A retailer sells a product at $650 per unit with stochastic demand following a known discrete distribution over 0 to 5 units. If demand exceeds available inventory, shortages are covered by purchasing from a competitor at $700 and reselling at $650, incurring a $50 loss per unit short. Ordering incurs a fixed cost of $50 plus $400 per unit ordered. Future profits are discounted at β = 0.9.
The state is the inventory level at the end of each week (after sales), ranging from 0 to 10 units. The decision is how many units to order, bringing inventory up to some order-up-to level s = i + j. The net reward combines expected revenue from sales, shortage penalty costs, and the ordering cost. Transition probabilities are derived from the demand distribution: the next-period inventory is max(0, s − d) for each demand realization d.
Value iteration initializes all state values to zero and repeatedly applies Bellman's equation: for each inventory level, every feasible order quantity is evaluated by summing the immediate net reward and the discounted expected future value under the current value function. The order quantity yielding the highest total is selected. Convergence is declared when the maximum value change across all states falls below 10⁻⁶.
The project is solved under two scenarios. Part (a) assumes no holding cost, finding the order-up-to levels that balance ordering costs against shortage penalties. Part (b) adds a holding cost of $30 per unit of leftover inventory per week, penalizing excess stock and shifting the optimal policy toward smaller, more frequent orders.
The output for each part includes convergence history, the final value function, optimal order quantities, net rewards, the transition probability matrix under the optimal policy, and a clean ordering policy summary. A side-by-side comparison then shows how adding holding costs changes order quantities at each inventory level.
